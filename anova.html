ANOVA & the F-Statistic

An interactive review for someone who needs the intuition and the formulas — fast.

1 · The question ANOVA answers

You have several groups and a single measurement per observation. Are the group means actually different, or is what you see just sampling noise?

A two-sample test (the t-test) handles two groups. ANOVA generalizes it to three or more, with one omnibus test. Here are three groups — eyeballing it, are they different?

2 · The one-sentence intuition

F = signal ÷ noise — specifically, the variance between group means divided by the variance within each group.

  • If groups are tightly clustered and far apart → big F → reject H₀.
  • If groups overlap a lot relative to how spread their means are → F ≈ 1 → fail to reject.

Big F: tight clusters, spread-apart means

F ≈ 1: noisy clusters, similar means

4 · The math, compactly

\(SS_T = \sum_{i,j}(x_{ij}-\bar{x})^2\)
Total variability around the grand mean.
\(SS_B = \sum_j n_j(\bar{x}_j-\bar{x})^2\)
Between-group: how far each group mean sits from the grand mean.
\(SS_W = \sum_{i,j}(x_{ij}-\bar{x}_j)^2\)
Within-group: leftover scatter inside each group.
\(SS_T = SS_B + SS_W\)
The variance partition — exact, not approximate.
\(MS_B = \frac{SS_B}{k-1},\quad MS_W = \frac{SS_W}{N-k}\)
Average squared deviation per degree of freedom.
\(F = \dfrac{MS_B}{MS_W} \sim F_{k-1,\,N-k}\) under H₀
Under H₀ (all means equal), both MS estimate σ². Their ratio sits near 1.

Step 1 — SSB (between-group)

\(SS_B = 4\big[(75-81)^2+(81-81)^2+(87-81)^2\big] = 4(36+0+36) = \mathbf{288}\)

Step 2 — SSW (within-group)

Group A deviations from 75: −3, +1, −1, +3 → squares 9+1+1+9 = 20
Group B deviations from 81: −1, +1, +3, −3 → squares 1+1+9+9 = 20
Group C deviations from 87: +1, −3, −1, +3 → squares 1+9+1+9 = 20
\(SS_W = 20 + 20 + 20 = \mathbf{60}\)

Step 3 — degrees of freedom

\(df_1 = k - 1 = 2\), \(df_2 = N - k = 12 - 3 = 9\)

Step 4 — mean squares and F

\(MS_B = 288/2 = 144\), \(MS_W = 60/9 \approx 6.667\)
\(F = 144 / 6.667 = \mathbf{21.6}\)

Step 5 — decision

Critical value \(F_{0.05,\,2,9} \approx 4.26\). Observed F = 21.6 ≫ 4.26 → reject H₀. p ≈ … (computed live by jStat below).

6 · Randomized Complete Block Design (RCBD)

Why blocking

If your experimental units differ on a known nuisance variable — soil quality, student ability, machine batch, time of day — that variability gets dumped into the error term and washes out your treatment signal. Block on the nuisance variable so its variance is recognized as a separate source instead of polluting error.

The model

\(y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_i + \beta_j + \varepsilon_{ij}\) — additive: each treatment shifts the mean by \(\tau_i\), each block by \(\beta_j\), and the residual is \(\varepsilon_{ij}\). One observation per cell, no treatment×block interaction.

The variance partition

  • \(SS_T = SS_{\text{Trt}} + SS_{\text{Blk}} + SS_E\)
  • df: treatment \(k-1\), block \(b-1\), error \((k-1)(b-1)\), total \(kb-1\)
  • Test of interest: \(F = MS_{\text{Trt}} / MS_E\), with \(F_{k-1,\,(k-1)(b-1)}\)

Step 1 — SS_Treatment

\(SS_{\text{Trt}} = b\sum_i(\bar{T}_i-\bar{x})^2 = 4[(7-10)^2+(10-10)^2+(13-10)^2] = 4(9+0+9) = \mathbf{72}\)

Step 2 — SS_Block

\(SS_{\text{Blk}} = k\sum_j(\bar{B}_j-\bar{x})^2 = 3[(11-10)^2+(9-10)^2+(13-10)^2+(7-10)^2] = 3(1+1+9+9) = \mathbf{60}\)

Step 3 — SS_Total

Sum of squared deviations of all 12 cells from the grand mean (10):

B1: (−3)²+2²+4² = 29 · B2: (−4)²+(−2)²+3² = 29 · B3: 1²+3²+5² = 35 · B4: (−6)²+(−3)²+0² = 45
\(SS_T = 29+29+35+45 = \mathbf{138}\)

Step 4 — SS_Error

\(SS_E = SS_T - SS_{\text{Trt}} - SS_{\text{Blk}} = 138 - 72 - 60 = \mathbf{6}\)

Sanity check: residual at cell (i,j) is \(e_{ij}=x_{ij}-\bar{T}_i-\bar{B}_j+\bar{x}\). All twelve are 0 or ±1; their squares sum to 6. ✓

Step 5 — degrees of freedom

Treatment: \(k-1=2\) · Block: \(b-1=3\) · Error: \((k-1)(b-1)=6\) · Total: \(kb-1=11\)

Step 6 — F and decision

\(MS_{\text{Trt}} = 72/2 = 36\), \(MS_E = 6/6 = 1\)
\(F = 36/1 = \mathbf{36}\), \(F_{0.05,\,2,6} \approx 5.14\) → reject H₀, p ≈ …

Blocking vs. ignoring blocks — same data, two analyses

Blocking moved 60 units of variance out of "error" and into a recognized source — same data, same treatment effect, but F jumped from 4.91 to 36. That's the entire reason RCBD exists: control nuisance variance to gain power.

When RCBD goes wrong

  • Missing cells break the partition (use a mixed model or expected-mean-squares approach).
  • Treatment×block interaction violates additivity — diagnose with a residual-vs-block plot.
  • Post-hoc blocking is cheating: blocks must be defined before you see outcomes.

7 · Why not just run multiple t-tests?

Family-wise error inflation. Each pairwise t-test has a 5% false-positive rate. Running \(\binom{k}{2}\) of them at α = 0.05 each gives an overall false-positive rate of about \(1 - 0.95^{\binom{k}{2}}\). For k=3 groups (3 tests): ≈ 14%. For k=5 (10 tests): ≈ 40%. ANOVA gives one omnibus test at the nominal α; if it's significant, then a controlled post-hoc procedure (Tukey HSD) tells you which pairs differ.

8 · Assumptions cheatsheet

  • Independence of observations (within and between groups).
  • Normality of residuals within each group — ANOVA is fairly robust unless n is small and distributions are wildly skewed.
  • Equal variances across groups (homoscedasticity). Check with Levene's test or plot residuals.
  • RCBD only: additivity — no treatment×block interaction.

9 · Exam traps

  • F is always ≥ 0 — it's a ratio of variances.
  • A significant ANOVA tells you something differs, not which pair. Post-hoc tests (Tukey HSD) come next.
  • One-way: one factor. Two-way: two factors crossed (with possible interaction). RCBD: one treatment factor, one nuisance factor, no interaction modeled.
  • A large F means strong evidence against H₀, not necessarily a large effect size — report η² or ω² for that.
  • In RCBD the F for blocks is usually printed but rarely the question of interest — blocks are there for variance control, not hypothesis testing.
